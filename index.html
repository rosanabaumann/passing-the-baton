About the Project

This project was developed as a Bachelor graduation project in Design Management at Lucerne University of Applied Sciences and Arts, in collaboration with the Swiss Center for Design and Health.

The research was conducted in the Emmental and Oberaargau region of Switzerland, in partnership with SOMEKO, GNE, GNO, COALA, and SCDH.

Each contributes to the same care situation, but still works from a different part of the system. This work is called invisible care coordination.

The System Challenge

Switzerland's healthcare system is highly capable, but fragmented. Care increasingly spans medical treatment, nursing support, social work, relatives, insurers, home care and institutions. When these actors work in parallel without a shared overview, care becomes vulnerable.

Macro
Macro — Health policy, financing, cantons, national strategies. The structural framework within which all care is organized.

Key Findings

01

Coordination already happens — but often invisibly, carried by motivated individuals rather than formal structures.

02

It is carried without formal mandate, protected time, or recognition.

03

Transitions are the most vulnerable moments. No single actor holds the full picture.

04

Information fragments across systems and institutions. Responsibility for the handover often remains unclear.

05

Coordination depends on trust, communication, shared knowledge and mutual respect.

06

SOMEKO needs visibility, shared language, clear entry points and recognition to become embedded.

07

The baton drops not because people don't care — but because the handover itself was never properly designed.

08

Making coordination visible is the first step toward making it shareable.

The Iceberg of Care Coordination

Hover over each item to learn more. The surface shows what is formally recognised. Below the waterline lives the work that keeps care together — invisible, unprotected, and unacknowledged.

What is visible

Official care meetings
Formal meetings between professionals — documented, scheduled, and recognized as part of the care process.
Formal referrals
Written referrals between GPs, specialists, and institutions that follow official pathways.
Documented handovers
Transfer letters, discharge reports, and formal transition documents.
Assigned coordination roles
Officially designated coordinators with a formal mandate — rare, but when present, highly effective.
waterline

What stays invisible

Informal phone calls between colleagues
Personal networks of trust that move information between systems — fast, but fragile and undocumented.
Personal contacts and workarounds
Knowing the right person to call, bending processes to make things work — invisible labour that fills structural gaps.
Repeated patient histories
Patients and families who tell the same story again and again to each new professional — a sign that information does not travel.
Coordination beyond formal role
Professionals who coordinate more than their job description requires — motivated by care, not mandate.
Emotional labour of keeping track
The cognitive and emotional weight of holding together a care situation no system formally owns.
Knowledge held in one person's head
Critical information about a patient that exists only in the memory of one professional — and disappears when they are absent.
Gaps in responsibility at transitions
The moment between discharge and intake where no one formally holds the picture — where the baton drops.

Impact & Strategy

The project does not claim to solve fragmentation alone. Instead, it creates conditions for care coordination to become more visible, shareable and discussable — working at the level of interactions, communication and handovers.
