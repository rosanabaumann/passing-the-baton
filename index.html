The problem is not that care coordination is missing; it is that it remains invisible, poorly understood, and therefore difficult to share.

01

Care coordination is already happening. Not through formal structures, but through invisible work carried by healthcare professionals, relatives, and volunteers.

02

Coordination already exists, but it cannot be recognised, discussed, shared, or improved because it remains invisible.

03

Everyone agrees integrated care is important. But when asked what it actually means in everyday practice, people struggle to answer.

The Swiss healthcare system works. But often because people coordinate beyond their formal roles. Each contributes to the same care situation, but still works from a different part of the system. This work is called invisible care coordination.

Care Coordination Across System Levels

Patient care does not emerge within a single organisation, but through the interaction of multiple system levels. At the macro level, policies, strategies, and funding structures shape the conditions of care. The meso level consists of regional networks and organisations that enable and coordinate collaboration across services. At the micro level, care takes place in practice—where patients, relatives, and healthcare professionals navigate everyday decisions and transitions.

The quality of care coordination depends on the interaction between all three levels. Care coordination therefore does not happen in one specific place, but in the handovers between levels—where information, responsibility, and decision-making are passed on.

Hover over each item to learn more. The surface shows what is formally recognised. Below the waterline lives the work that keeps care together — invisible, unprotected, and unacknowledged.

What is visible

Official care meetings
Formal meetings between professionals — documented, scheduled, and recognized as part of the care process.
Formal referrals
Written referrals between GPs, specialists, and institutions that follow official pathways.
Documented handovers
Transfer letters, discharge reports, and formal transition documents.
Assigned coordination roles
Officially designated coordinators with a formal mandate — rare, but when present, highly effective.
waterline

What stays invisible

Informal phone calls between colleagues
Personal networks of trust that move information between systems — fast, but fragile and undocumented.
Personal contacts and workarounds
Knowing the right person to call, bending processes to make things work — invisible labour that fills structural gaps.
Repeated patient histories
Patients and families who tell the same story again and again to each new professional — a sign that information does not travel.
Coordination beyond formal role
Professionals who coordinate more than their job description requires — motivated by care, not mandate.
Emotional labour of keeping track
The cognitive and emotional weight of holding together a care situation no system formally owns.
Knowledge held in one person's head
Critical information about a patient that exists only in the memory of one professional — and disappears when they are absent.
Gaps in responsibility at transitions
The moment between discharge and intake where no one formally holds the picture — where the baton drops.

Coordination is Care.

The project does not claim to solve fragmentation by giving a tool or a product that strictly needs to be used by everyone. Instead it creates conditions for care coordination to become something people can recognise, discuss, and deliberately design for — working at the level of interactions, communication and handovers.
